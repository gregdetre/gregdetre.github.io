Huntington disease

a hyperkinetic motor disorder

5-10 men/women per 100,000, usually middle-aged people

characterized by:

heritability

if the first exon of the huntingtin gene has >40 repeats of the trinucleotide sequence CAG

earlier onset in the offspring than the parent ('anticipation')

chorea (see below)

behavioral/psychiatric disturbances

cognitive impairement/dementia

death 15-20 years after onset

see:

basal ganglia

Parkinson disease

causation:

widespread loss of neurons in the brain

seen earliest in the striatum

both GABA and acetylcholine neurons affected

dopamine/acetylcholine ratio is too high

seems to affect the indirect pathway more

inhibition of external globus pallidus reduced

causing the globus pallidus to be more active

inhibits the subthalamic nucleus

this inactivation of the subthalamic nucleus may causes the symptoms that look like hemiballismus

too much disinhibition of the thalamus and too much thalamic activity -> hyperkinetic disorder

glutamate#excitotoxicity may also be a factor (ks4 866)

the altered gene on chromosome 4 may lead to excessive activation of NMDA receptors or release of glutamate

Symptoms

see causation for mechanisms

choreiform/athetoid movements and dementia. Progressive. Movement disorders begin in distal extremities but ends up affecting the entire body.
