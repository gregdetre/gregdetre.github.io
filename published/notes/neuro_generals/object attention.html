Early vs late processing bottleneck

feature integration theory - argues for early bottleneck

Rensink & Enns, 1998

argues against early capacity limitations (as proposed by feature integration theory)

search for oddly-shaped object (looks like square occluded by circle) (cf fn2 1265)

pops out among squares and circles

however, does not pop out among squares and circles when a circle fits right into the occluded part

implies that the visual system has already completed it into a square at an early stage, before visual search

Duncan (1984)

showed that you're much better at two judgements about features from the same object than about different objects

so when attending to one feature of oan object, all fo the features that make up the objet can automatically be selected together becaues they're already linked otgether into objects

suggests a late bottleneck

Topdown biasing (Chelazzi et al, 1998)

showed that many TE neurons had a higher baseline firing rate during the day period when the cue stimulus was a preferred stimulus for the cell than when a poor stimulus

could be biasing towards detecting the target

Receptive fields

size of RFs increases as you go down the ventral stream (Ungerleider & Mishkin, 1982)

from <1deg in V1 to 20deg in TE

same for dorsal pathway

neurons can't send information about all of the stimuli in their RFs at once

which is why the processing limitations are more obvious at higher levels

kastner et al, (1998) - competition in imaging

sequential vs simultaneous condition

activity in V1 was the same in simultaneous and sequential presentation

but activity in V2, and especially V4 and TEO, was reduced for simultaneous

mutual suppression

scaled to the size of the receptive fields

this mutual suppression could be partially cancelled out by attending covertly to the stimulus location

strongest reduction in suppression in V4 and TEO
