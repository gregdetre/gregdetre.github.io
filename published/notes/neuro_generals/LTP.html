LTP

Aka

long-term potentiation

Part of

learning

Definition

potentiation = increase in the measured depolarization or excitation delivered by a controlled stimulus onto a receiving neuron

long-term potentiation = long-lasting enhancement in efficacy of the synapse between two neurons

Details

LTP (Bliss & Lomo, 1973)

first showed it in an electrode in CA1, stimulated CA3

high-frequency 100Hz stimulation for 1s

causes long-lasting potentiation effect, with increases in synaptic efficacy of 50-100% or more

activation of 100Hz for 1s does not happen all that often in the cortex. probably also other factors:

  • timing
  • intensity
  • frequency
  • duration

    some studies showing behaviorally-measured learning despite NMDA blockade - though the NMDA-blocking drugs have significant other effects on behavior (Keith & Rudy, 1990)

NMDA-mediated LTP/D

most common

NMDA receptor depends on both

presynaptic activity

need it to release glutamate, which then needs to be bound to the receptor

postsynaptic activity

postsynaptic membrane potential must be excited enough to cause magnesium (Mg++) ions to move out of the opening of the NMDA receptor channel (which they otherwise block)

once the NMDA channels open

allow Ca++ ions to enter the postsynaptic neuron

makes a big difference

triggers a complex cascade of chemical processes, eventually resulting in a change of synaptic efficacy of the AMPA (primary excitatory) input receptors

Role of other receptors/channels in LTP/LTD

involvement of mGlu receptors in LTP (Bear & Malenka, 1994)

other ways that calcium can enter the postsynaptic neuron (e.g. non-NMDA voltage-dependent calcium channels)

LTP/LTD regulated by a number of other modulatory neurotransmitters (e.g. dopamine, serotonin) - not clear how

From wikipedia

NMDA-receptor in CA1 hippocampus = prototypical site of mammalian LTP study

other hippocampal LTP depends on mGlu

occurs in

"Since its original discovery in the rabbit hippocampus, LTP has been observed in a variety of other neural structures, including the cerebral cortex, cerebellum, amygdala, and many others. Robert Malenka, a prominent LTP researcher, has suggested that LTP may even occur at all excitatory synapses in the mammalian brain."

two phases

early LTP (E-LTP) = early protein synthesis-independent phase

late LTP (L-LTP) = late protein synthesis-dependent phase

From KS4

classical conditioning (ks4 1253/4)

large increase in transmitter release

requires three signals in a siphon sensory neuron to converge

  • activation of adenylyl cyclase by C++ influx - conditioned stimulus
  • activation of serotonergic receptors coupled to adenylyl cyclase - unconditioned stimulus
  • a retrograde signal indicating postsynaptic cell has been adequately activated by the unconditioned stimulus
