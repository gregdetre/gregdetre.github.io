Definition

drug addiction = substance dependence (???)

addiction = physical dependence (???)

reinforcement and motivation are crucial parts of substance dependence

tolerance = loss of an effect of a drug with repeated administration

withdrawal = the appearance of symptoms associated with the termination of chronic drug abuse.

self administration: level pressing, intravenous vs. oral self administration.

measured with dose response curves

also brain stimulation. Drugs of abuse make brain stimulation more rewarding, or decrease thresholds for ICSS. Also: place conditioning. Animals return to place where drug was last received.

positive vs negative reinforcement:

initial rush/good feeling of drug is positive reinforcement

withdrawal symptoms are negative reinforcement

motivated behavior: involves areas of midbrain and forebrain and connections between both through medial forebrain bundle. Brain monoamine systems travel through here. VTA (in midbrain)- basal forebrain (NuAcc, olfactory tubercle, frontal cortex, amygdala), and DA connections between the VTA and Basal Forebrain (Mesolimbic DA system). Also: opioid peptide systems, GABA systems, and serotonin systems involved with DA.

Cocaine/Amphetamine

Adding this to system increases the amount of DA, NE and 5-HT in the synapse. They do this by blocking reuptake or enhancing release of neurotransmitters. But reinforcing effects depend critically on DA. Mesolimbic DA system especially implicated. DA antagonists into NuAcc, amygdala block cocaine and amphetamine self-administration in rats.

Particularly important for reinforcing action of cocaine: shell of NuAcc and central amygdaloid nucleus

Opioids

Discovery of endogenous opioids and opioid receptors in the brain. Opioid antagonists block heroin self-administration when drugs injected into the VTA and NuAcc. But DA antagonists do not block heroin self-administration. Both regions are important for reinforcing opioid dependence, but both DA- dependant and DA-independent opioid mechanisms are important.

Nicotine: agonist at nicotinic acytlcholine receptors in brain. Three neurotransmitter systems are involved: opioid, cholinergic, and DA systems. Nicoline activateds both the mesolimbic DA system and endogenous opioid systems.

Alcohol: GABA has a role in alcohol abuse? GABA antagonists reverse behavioral effects of alcohol, and also decrease alcohol reinforcement. Effects central amygdaloid nucleus. DA receptor antagonists work in the same way. However, destroying DA terminals does not stop self-administration of alcohol. DA transmission not absolutely critical. Host of other chemicals are involved: glutamate, 5-HT, opioids. Increase in 5-HT decreases voluntary intake of alcohol. Opioid antagonists (naltrexone, naloxone) reduce self-administration of alcohol.

THC: binds to cannvinoid-1 receptor, found throughout the brain, especially in basal ganglia. THC increases DA in the shell of the NuAcc.

Extended Amygdala: shell of NuAcc extends into centromedial amygdala. Sends projections to palladium, VTA, reticular formation, raphe nucli, LH. Connections between the extended amygdala and the hypothalamus. Key between drug reward and natural reward mechanisms.

Chronic drug administration compromises the brain reward systems. Withdrawal from prolonged self-administration of cocaine in rats increases ICSS reward thresholds. Naloxone is an opiate antagonist increases brain stimulation.

Increases in drugs, decreases brain stimulation. Decreases in drugs/withdrawal, increases brain stimulation.

Neurochemical adaptation in reward neurotransmitters. During alcohol, opiate, and cocaine withdrawal, extracellular DA and 5-HT levels in NuAcc decrease.*

Also, there are DA postsynaptic changes in the NuAcc. Long term changes in receptor function in mesolimbic DA system.

Stress increases with drug abuse and withdrawal. Brain CRF levels rise, esp. in central amygdaloid nucleus. Stress chemicals (CRF/NE in locus ceruleus) rise in limbic forebrain with chronic drug use

Opponent process model (Soloman, 1977): initial hedonic state associated with drug, followed by negative affective state. With time, hedonic state becomes smaller and smaller, while negative affective state (affective withdrawal) becomes larger and larger. We see this in brain: with chronic drug use, decreased function of reward neurotransmitters (DA) and increased brain stress neurotransmitters (NE, CRF).

Neuroadaptive processes underlie substance dependence: Is drug dependence due to positive reinforcing effects of drug or due to the negative reinforcing effects of withdrawal symptoms? Sensitization theory vs. neuroadaptation theory. Sensitization theory: drug causes sensitization of the mesolimbic DA system, and therefore creates craving. Neuroadaptation theory: (ex- opponent process theory) continued drug use lead to neuroadaptation to the presence of the drug, leads to development of a negative affective states, and leads to self-medication of the affective state. Allostasis: both theories work together to create addiction.
