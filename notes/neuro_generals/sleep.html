Characterized by

  • reduced in voluntary body movement
  • stereotypic postures
  • decreased reaction to external stimuli
  • relatively easy reversibility
  • also:
    • an increased rate of anabolism (the synthesis of cell structures)
    • and a decreased rate of catabolism (the breakdown of cell structures)

Awake state

dominant - alpha waves c. 10Hz

Stage 1

near-disappearance of alpha waves

theta waves - low amplitude, desynchronised

not clear gradation from wakefulness - more like quietly resting

'somnolence' or 'drowsy sleep'

Stage 2

sleep spindles - bursts of activity in the EEG lasting c. half a second, c. 12Hz, very irregular

generated by rhythmic firing of thalamic relay neurons

result from GABA-ergic inhibitory neurons in the nucleus reticularis (forms a shell around the thalamus)

novel type of action potential - hyperpolarized, calcium influx, burst of action potentials, back to hyperpolarized again

seems to occlude transmission of sensory information through thalamus and cortex

c. 50% of total sleep

Stage 3

mainly a transition into stage 4

part of slow wave sleep

delta waves - c. 0.5-2Hz

3-8% of total sleep time

Stage 4

intensification of stage 3

true delta sleep

10-15% of total sleep time

difficult to wake from

night terrors, bedwetting and sleepwalking occur in this stage

see REM sleep

boost immune system

In animals

small mammals sleep more than large mammals

bigger brains have longer REM/non-REM cycles

mammals born immature have more REM sleep than precocial mammals

some marine animals and some birds show non-REM sleep patterns in only one cerebral hemisphere at a time

birds' sleep episodes are much shorter

muscle atonia during REM is rare

heritability of sleep patterns demonstrated within species

total sleep time, REM sleep and circadian rhythms may be inherited independently
