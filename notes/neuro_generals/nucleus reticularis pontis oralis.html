PGO-on cells

fire in bursts

initiate PGO spikes in lateral geniculate nucleus

destruction

blocks PGO spikes but not the rest of REM

stimulation

produces PGO spikes even outside of REMe

regulated by the REM-off cells in the raphe nuclei

REM-waking-on cells

they fire:

during active waking

burst firing mediates movement of the head, neck, limbs and eyes

during REM

rapid eye movements and muscle twitches

at lower rates during non-REM sleep

from another source: many excite motor neurons, others control the EEG

REM-on cells

not very many, but important in REM

they fire:

a lot during REM

very little during waking or non-REM

subtypes:

GABA-ergic - inhibit activity in serotonergic and noradrenergic (which???) cells during REM sleep

glutamatergic REM-on cells responsible for the loss of muscle tone in REM sleep

abnormal activity in the REM-on cells an cause a sudden loss of muscle tone (cateaplexy(, one of the primary symptoms of narcoleps

excessive activity during sleep contributes to sleep apnea (collapse of the airway from reduced muscle tone)

insufficient activity can cause REM sleep behavior disorder (a release of motor activity during REM sleep, during which dreams are acted out)
