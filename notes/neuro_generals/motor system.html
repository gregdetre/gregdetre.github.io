Medial nuclei (in ventromedial motor nucleus) of spinal cord contain the motor neurons innervating axial muscles of the neck and back

Lateral nuclei (in dorsolateral motor nucleus) in the spinal cord: most medial neurons innervate proximal muscles while the most lateral innervate distal muscles.

Axons of medial propriospinal neurons run in the ventral and medial columns. They have long axons that branch. They coordinate movements for the neck and pelvis.

Voluntary Movement: Chapter 38, Kandel

Voluntary movements differ from reflexes in a number of important ways: 1. Voluntary movements are organized around performance of purposeful task. 2. Experience and learning can influence effectiveness of voluntary task. 3. Voluntary movments are not just responses to environmental stimuli. Rather, they are generated internally.

Motor cortical areas are organized in a rough somatotopic organization. Face and fingers in human motor cortex have a much larger representation than monkeys. Face, hands, and fingers are overrepresented than other body parts. Medial: feet (and genitals?). Map moves progressively over the body, ending with the face and tongue most laterally. Need more fine motor control for these areas, hence they are represented more in cortex.

We know about these motor maps from electrical stimulation in the human (Penfield) and monkeys. From intracortical microstimulation, we know that neurons in several cortical sites project to axons to the same target.

Different areas of motor cortex send projects to the same motor neurons, and thus, exert control** Also, most stimuli activate several muscles, so there is not a one to one mapping***

Motor maps: there is a redundancy in muscle representation. Why? So inputs to motor cortex form other cortical areas can combine proximal and distal muscles in different ways in different tasks.
