Overview

at rest

myosin heads in cocked position

troponin-tropomyosin on thin filaments

have no bound calcium

are blocking binding sites on actin

muscle fiber is activated

calcium is released from sarcoplasmic reticulum

binds to some of the tropomyosin

changes thin filament conformation

unblocks the actin-binding sites

cross bridge power stroke

myosin heads from the thick filaments can now attach to the thin filaments (= cross bridge)

myosin heads rotate

pulls thick filaments and thin filaments into greater overlap, i.e. ratchets

shortening muscle fiber

fresh ATP binds to myosin head, which detaches

releases chemical energy

recock myosin head, ready for next power stroke

without ATP, you get rigor mortis

Details

thin filaments and thick filaments slide past each other

ATPase on thick filaments converts ATP into mechanical energy -> cocked deformation

thin filaments are activated by calcium

myosin head attaches to a binding site on the thin filament

stored mechanical energy is released

once the thick filament attaches to the thin filament, they create a cross bridge

acts like an oar, pulling the thin filament in a direction that increases the overlap between thin filaments and thick filaments

shortens the muscle fiber

after a sliding motion, the stress in the cross bridge is relieved

and it can detach

head is then recocked for attachment to another binding site

attachment, rotation and detachment need ADP, ATP and calcium
