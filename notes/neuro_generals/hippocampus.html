however, there is contradictory evidence that it is involved in short-term memory (Holdstock et al, 1995; Buffalo et al, 1998)

Rats with fornix lesions could learn the location of a hidden platform in a water maze when each trial began from the same starting point. However, unlike normal rats, the operated animals failed to find the platform when they were started from a new direction. The normal rats succeeded because they had acquired a relational memory that represented the platform location in the context of various room cues. The operated animals had learned a habit, a fixed path from a single starting point to the goal location.

In one notable study from Eichenbaum's laboratory, a cup was placed randomly in one of nine locations in an open field. The cup contained an odor and, if the odor differed from the odor presented on the previous trial, the rat could find a reward. Half of the neurons that exhibited task-related activity fired independently of spatial location, and most of the cells sensitive to location were not exclusively spatial. That is, the activity of these cells was typically associated with the conjunction of location and one or more other task-related features (for example, odor or goal-approach movements).

The hippocampus is a scrolled structure located in the medial temporal lobe

The hippocampus can be divided into at least five different areas, as labeled above. The dentate gyrus is the dense dark layer of cells at the "tip" of the hippocampus. Areas CA3 and CA1 are more diffuse; the small CA2 is hard to distinguish between them. (CA stands for cornu ammonis, from its ram's horn shape.) The subiculum sits at the base of the hippocampus, and is continuous with entorhinal cortex, which is part of the parahippocampal gyrus. There is essentially a one-way flow of information through the hippocampus, as diagrammed below.

Information enters the hippocampus by jumping across what appears to be a gap between the subiculum and dentate gyrus. This tract is called the perforant path, as it perforates the space between the two. The entorhinal axons then synapse on cells in the dentate gyrus. The dentate neurons, in turn, send axons to CA3; these are called mossy fibers. ("Mossy fibers" is a morphological description for axons with large bulbous terminals, and these are unrelated to those in the cerebellum.) CA3 sends axons called Schaeffer collaterals to CA1, which sends yet another set of fibers to the subiculum. The subiculum is responsible for the output of the hippocampus: it can either send axons directly to the hypothalamus and mammillary bodies via the fornix (remember the fornix?), or it can pass along the information back to entorhinal cortex, which will relay it all back to sensory cortex. It is essentially one continuous pathway that begins in sensory cortex, traverses the hippocampus (loop-the-loop), and returns to sensory cortex.

Hasselmo encoding/retrieval modes

Hasselmo dumped acetylcholine on hippocampal slices and looked at the strength of different projections

argues for two modes

rapidly flipping back and forth between encoding mode and retrieval mode

Retrieval mode

acetylcholine absent

less learning, strong projections

turn off dentate gyrus -> CA3

weakens the CA3 projections

Encoding mode

acetylcholine present

increased potentiation (to cause binding)
